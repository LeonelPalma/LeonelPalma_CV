Books That Made My Financial Life Easier

Through the experiences and teachings of others, we can gain enough knowledge and ideas to change the course of our lives.

Continuously learning and developing new skills will greatly increase the chances of achieving lasting success, not only through mindset change, adopting good financial habits and investing regularly, but also increased productivity, marketing techniques and entrepreneurship lessons.

Obviously it's not an easy task, it requires some sacrifices, a lot of dedication, work and even a pinch of luck, so to speak, so that when the opportunity comes you can be alert to recognize it and prepared to take advantage of it.

So, I share some of the books I read that made my life easier, and if it makes sense to you, they can change yours too.


1) Rich Dad Poor Dad (Robert T. Kiyosaki and Sharon L. Lechter)

This was the first book to speak clearly about financial independence and show you the way to go from being an employee to being an investor. First published in 1997 in the United States, the book became an immediate best seller and today enjoys the status of a classic in financial education literature. The authors use parables based on Kiyosaki's life to indicate efficient ways to build heritage. The work generated a series of other products, such as publications and games aimed at children and adolescents.

2) Secrets of the Millionaire Mind (T. Harv Eker)

After talking about one of the greatest classics about financial education, I present one of the greatest classics about personal development mindset. This book makes comparisons between a poverty-oriented mind and a wealth-oriented mind, presenting the main mindset shifts you need to put in place if you want to think like a rich person. Eker tells how he managed to overcome personal difficulties and become a millionaire in two and a half years. Eker's method involves 17 basic principles that should guide a person towards the accumulation of wealth.

3) Work 4 Hours a Week (Timothy Ferriss)

This book is one of the greatest classics of digital entrepreneurship and, without a doubt, is the greatest inspiration to quit a job and dedicate exclusively to digital business. In this book, you will learn about a new concept of work, retirement and lifestyle. The work is a true manual for entrepreneurs with a focus on high productivity. Ferris seeks to clarify the relationship between time and money, bringing important reflections. The proposal is to forget the old concept of work and not wait for retirement to enjoy life. Whether your dream is to escape the rut, experience great world travel, earn a five-figure monthly income, or just live longer and work less, âWork 4 Hours a Weekâ is the book for you.

4) The Millionaire Next Door (Thomas J. Stanley and William D. Danko)

This is a little-talked-about book about the true habits of wealthy Americans. In this book, you'll learn the top common habits of first-generation millionaires who built wealth on their own, not through inheritance.

5) The Intelligent Investor (Benjamin Graham)

This book is one of the greatest classics (perhaps even the greatest) in the stock market investing literature and has been cited as the greatest investment book in history by none other than Warren Buffett (the greatest investor in the world). In this book, you will learn fundamental concepts about how to invest in the stock market and how to build your investment portfolio. To illustrate these concepts, the author uses reports of historical events, that is, real examples that help investors understand the topic.

6) Think And Grow Rich (Napoleon Hill)

It is one of the greatest classics on leadership and personal development. In this book, you will learn a new philosophy of leadership, self-motivation and personal success. The author spent 20 years investigating the methods and practices of 500 of the world's richest men in the early decades of the 20th century. From this research, the author extracted 15 characteristics common to all those who amassed a fortune. 7) Fishing for Fools â The Economics of Manipulation and Fraud (George A. Akerlof and Robert J. Shiller)

Written by award-winning and renowned economists George Akerlof and Robert Shiller, this book addresses the pitfalls of the financial market and shows how they are used to exploit psychological weaknesses and consumer ignorance. Among the topics discussed are: the way in which the credit card is used, often without limits; and the seductive tactics of advertising campaigns, which make us pay dearly for the acquisition of some goods. The book also includes stories of characters who knew how to deal with these manipulations by seeking more knowledge, demonstrating tactics on how to escape these consumption traps. This work is essential for controlling expenses and purchases, because once you start to recognize the pitfalls, you start to fall into them less.

8) The Richest Man in Babylon (George S. Clason)

This book shows that ordinary people can build wealth from scratch, and that for that to happen, it is enough to change habits and thoughts. This is the perfect work to give you the push you needed to start your investments.
